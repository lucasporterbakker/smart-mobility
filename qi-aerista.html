Thoughts and Feedback


Summary:

In general, I feel that the site has too much friction. I would probably recommend starting with a short product sprint workshop with this group, to clarify the unique value proposition (UVP) and refine the language used on the site — working towards communicating the core journey in 10% of what is currently there. Having spent two hours with them on this, I would be able to work with them to implement the changes, or code a new site from scratch, in less than a week depending on feedback. Killing this friction, implementing some technical tools to monitor user engagement, and testing some new strategies will help get their sales pipeline running smoothly.


Things that are Good:

The subscription model for ordering tea is ideal, as one of the most successful business models for software is automated recurring payments. I would, recommend merging the ‘The Tea Club’ and ‘Teas’ tabs, and offer the subscription model as a way to bring the cost down; example: scaling discounts. Some A / B testing with users might be interesting here.

Having the call to action (CTA) right up front is good. I would, however, enlarge it and refine the language; example: Order Now. A hero tagline is also good, but I would consider refining it as well. At present, it repeats the CTA; examples: Smart Tea, A New Way to Brew Tea, etc.

The ‘How Qi Aerista Works’ video communicates the product more effectively than the language currently used on the site. I would recommend drawing on that for inspiration. However, the video should be embedded (not take me away from their sales pipeline into YouTube). A bonus would be meta data to switch servers depending on geography; example: Youku <> Vimeo.


Areas to Improve:

The ‘download app’ CTA could be elsewhere, as this fragments the sales funnel.

In general, the use case is unclear. It seems at first like a portal to buy a better tea-pot, but turns into a tea marketplace.

Some of the images show people enjoying tea using traditional brewing methods, this detracts from the inconvenience they are trying to convey.

The awards design section could be condensed from an entire section to a small strip, or have logos added to a banner image.

There are five sections dedicated to ‘how it works,’ this needs to be simplified. It could be one section with a clean flow diagram, for example.

When clicking-through on ‘Shop Brewer,’ I am showed a power adapter. This psychologically communicates inconvenience. I agree that adding a bonus gift can be an effective nudge, but I wouldn’t mention it before the transaction is complete.

There are three different font-faces on the landing page alone, two at most would be ideal. Additionally, they could consider removing the dependency on Google Fonts - the site has Roboto and Montserrat currently - this increases the website’s loading time (in markets like China) by 10X or more.

Overall, the UI needs some work.

The newsletter section at the bottom could be modal instead; example drop-down: Want to get notified of great deals?

I would recommend WeChat integration. I could help with this.

The seven images feel like an infomercial and connote low-quality.

Feedback sections can be effective for new products, but I might suggest condensing it and doing a quick fix on the UI.


General Advice:

It’s almost as if there are two business models here, one is a recurring purchase at a lower amount (tea), and the other a one-time more expensive purchase (the brewer).

I would recommend spending less space and valuable user attention-span trying to establish that there is a pain point. If users have it, they have it. Really, the product is about making a great thing even better. This could be communicated in one small section, instead of several.

The landing page is a lengthy infinite scroll - I counted 15+ sections. This could be cut in half.

The checkout flow feels confusing to a new user. Having detailed information is good customer service, but I might display say, 20% of that; and have an ‘expand tab’ for users who want more. This could also be a tooltip or popover. The payment pipeline should feel clean and easy, and showing all of the product specs info halfway through the payment flow makes me reconsider.

Generally speaking, a core user journey should have no more than five views maximum. But with a site like this - where users don’t need search functionality (to look for a specific product among many), or to be able to browse through different products before selecting one - there should be even fewer. I would aim for three.
